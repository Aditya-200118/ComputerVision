\section{Introduction and Problem Statement}

% Describe the "Why". What is the gap in current computer vision research or applications?
Hyperspectral imaging (HSI) is an advanced optical sensing technique that assembles spectroscopy and digital photography into a single system  \parencite{hong2026hyperspectralimaging}. The imaging collects and processes scene information by dividing the whole spectrum into tens of hundreds of bands, typically covering wavelengths from 380 nm to 2500 nm \parencite{Rybicki:847173}. The technique has been widely adopted in many fields, e.g., medical diagnosis, healthcare, remote sensing and identifying foreign objects in food products \parencite{ghimpeteanu2025hyperspectralimagingidentifyingforeign}. The hyperspectral images possess a three-dimensional structure, comprising two dimensions of spatial information and one dimension of spectral information. However, conventional spectrometers often operate in 1D line or 2D plane scanning, which is time-consuming and not suitable for dynamic scenes. Also, the devices for acquiring the HSIs are typically more complicated and expensive than common cameras. 
% In order to obtain the spectral information more effectively by extracting the required spectral information from the RGB images. 
The need to reconstruct the spectral properties of scene reflectance (and illumination) from a three-channel device or standard color space was recognized as early as 1980s \parencite{Hall1988IlluminationAC,Jaaskelainen:90, Maloney:86, Maloney:86, Parkkinen:89}. But this is an extremely ill-posed problem since a large amount of information is lost during the process of spectral integration when RGB sensors capture the light.\\ Spectral reconstruction from RGB images often suffer from a \textit{metameric} dilemma, where distinct spectral distribution map to nearly identical RGB values, making them indistinguishable. Recent approaches involve deep learning methods to solve this by learning the mapping from RGB to HSI. While these models achieve reasonable quantitative metrics, they operate as black boxes that ignore the underlying mechanism behind the imaging process. To overcome these issues, the goal is to incorporate physics based formulations into the architecture itself. By explicitly embedding the camera's Spectral Response Function (SRF) as a mathematical forward-model constraint, the network is forced to output hyperspectral reconstructions that strictly adhere to the physical laws of image formation, achieving both higher accuracy and reliable material distinction.

\section{Literature Review}
A RGB image is usually generated in three wide spectral channels with Bayer Filters, which is essentially a color filter array (CFA) placed over digital image sensor to capture color information. While, an HSI is obtained by the interaction between the spectral reflectance and illumination. The relation between the RGB and HSI images can be represented by

\begin{equation}
    I_c(x, y) = \int_{w_1}^{w_2} R(x, y, w) L(w) S_c(w) \, dw
\end{equation}
where $I_c$ represents each channel of RGB image, $S_c(w)$ denotes the spectral response function and $R(x,y,z)$ is the spectral reflectance of the objects and $L(w)$ is the illumination spectrum. HSI is typically defined as
\begin{equation}
    I_c(x, y) = \int_{w_1}^{w_2} H(x, y, w) S_c(w) \, dw
\end{equation}
where, $H(x,y,z)$ represents the Hyperspectral image. The challenge in spectral reconstruction is to invert the above forward image formation model in optimization framework to find the $H(x, y, z)$. A wide variety of methods have been developed to achieve spectral reconstruction.

\subsection{Prior-Based Methods}
These methods rely on mathematical assumptions around which the algorithms are designed. Broadly there are three categories of Prior-Based methods namely dictionary learning, manifold learning and gaussian process. \textcite{Arad2016SparseRO} introduced a method to reconstruct HSI directly from RGB leveraging hyperspectral priors in order to create a sparse dictionary of hyperspectral signatures and their corresponding RGB projections. The method works by collecting the hyperspectral prior, preferably from a set of domain specific scenes. This prior is then reduced computationally to an overcomplete dictionary $D_h$ of hyperspectral signatures. The dictionary is projected to the sensor space via the receptor spectral absorbance functions $R$, establishing a corresponding RGB dictionary, $D_{rgb}$. 
This correspondence between each RGB vector and its hyperspectral signature originator $h_i$ is maintained for the later mapping from RGB to hyperspectral signatures. For a given image, a sparse weight vector $w$ is calculated, that reconstructs a given RGB pixel query from the RGB dictionary. Because both dictionaries share the exact same sparse coefficients, applying these weights back to the hyperspectral dictionary yields the estimated spectrum signature $h_q$. 

While, manifold learning approach is aimed at finding a low-dimensional manifold that can uniquely represent the high dimensional data. \textcite{8237766} proposed a manifold approach but simplifying the three to many mapping into a well conditioned three to three problem using a isometric mapping algorithm which is used to reduce dimensionality of the basis spectra to a three-dimensional subspace. After finding a low dimensional embedding of natural scene spectra, a non linear mapping is learned between the RGB vectors and their corresponding 3D embedded natural scene spectra using a radial basis function with 10 hidden neurons and leveraging Levenberg-Marquardt training algorithm \parencite{Levenberg1944AMF,Marquardt1963AnAF}. The reconstruction of high dimensional spectrum from its 3D embedding employs a dictionary learning-based technique, essentially finding a common coding between the 3D embedding and high dimensional space of the spectra.

\textcite{akhtar2018hyperspectralrecoveryrgbimages} proposed a technique to estimate the HSI from RGB by modeling natural spectra with Gaussian processes. By exploiting the kernels of gaussian process to model the relative smoothness of naturally occurring spectra and accounting for the non-negativity of reflectance values and the self-similarity of hyperspectral image patches for accurate spectral recovery.
% this formulation inherently guarantees that the reconstructed hyperspectral signal is physically consistent with the original RGB measurement (i.e., $c_q = R \cdot h_q$), though the overall reconstruction quality remains strictly bounded by the representational capacity of the initial hyperspectral prior.

\subsection{Deep-Learning Methods}
\cite{Xiong2017HSCNNCH} introduced a deep learning approach to recover the hyperspectral image. The method first applies a upsampling matrix to interpolate the 3-channel input into a high-dimensional spectral volume. A convolutional network, inspired by the residual learning architectures like VSDR, is then applied to the the volume. The network learns a learns and end-end to mapping from a large number of upsampled/ground truth hyperspectral image pairs.

Building upon the original HSCNN, the advanced HSCNN+ \parencite{Shi2018HSCNNAC} models eliminate the need for hand-crafted upsampling and significantly improve feature extraction.
Instead of using a fixed interpolation matrix, HSCNN+ utilizes a pointwise convolutional layer to directly expand the 3-channel RGB input into the targeted spectral dimensions and replacing the plain convolution layers in HSCNN by modern residual blocks to boost its performance allowing the the network to learn the optimal spectral mapping.

\textcite{cai2022mstmultistagespectralwisetransformer} introduced Vision Transformer based architecture shifting from CNN-based methods. Excelling at capturing long-range dependencies in hyperspectral data, MST$++$ employs a novel spectral-wise multi head self-attention (S-MSA) that is based on the hyperspectral characteristics. Which allows the model to capture global spatial context and complex spectral correlations simultaneously, which traditional CNNs struggle to achieve due to their localized receptive fields

\subsection{Proposed Algorithm/Model Architecture}

To bridge the gap between deep learning based mapping and the physical reality of the imaging process of images, I propose a Physics-Informed architecture. Moving beyond standard direct mapping by structurally embedding physical priors and utilizing metameric data augmentation. It consists of a physical baseline estimation, a deep feature extraction backbone for discrepancy modeling, and a differentiable forward-model constraint layer.

\subsubsection{Training Data Curation: Metameric Augmentation}
Standard datasets lack sufficient metameric diversity, causing the networks to easily overfit to the brightness and shape. To mitigate this, I introduce a metameric augmentation step during training. Following metameric black theory, the ground truth spectrum $H_{gt}$ is decomposed into a fundamental metamer $S^*$ and an invisible metameric black component $B$. A physically valid augmented ground truth $H'_{gt}$ is generated by scaling the metameric black by a random coefficient $\alpha$:
$$H'_{gt} = S^* + \alpha B$$
This step trains the network to explicitly recognize the underdetermined nature of the metamer problem.

\subsubsection{Discrepancy Modeling Network}
Instead of blindly mapping from RGB to a full $C$-channel hyperspectral volume from scratch, the pipeline employs a discrepancy modeling approach. First, a mathematically guaranteed baseline $H_{baseline} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$ is calculated using the pseudo-inverse of the camera's Spectral Response Function (SRF), denoted as $\Phi^{\dagger}$:
$$H_{baseline} = I_{rgb} \Phi^{\dagger}$$
The deep neural network, acting as the discrepancy mapping function $f_\theta$, takes $I_{rgb}$ and predicts only the missing high-frequency residual details $\Delta H \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$. I plan to evaluate reliable backbones such as U-Net or ResNet (as well as lightweight Vision Transformers) for this stage. The final predicted hyperspectral volume is the sum of the physical baseline and the learned discrepancy:
$$\hat{H} = H_{baseline} + \Delta H$$

\subsubsection{Differentiable Forward Model Layer (Physics Enforcer)}
To eliminate the black-box nature of standard predictions, a parameter-free, differentiable layer mathematically simulates the camera's sensor integration. By discretizing the forward model, we project the predicted hyperspectral image $\hat{H}$ back into the RGB domain to generate a simulated RGB image, $\hat{I}_{rgb}$, using the known SRF projection matrix $\Phi \in \mathbb{R}^{C \times 3}$:
$$\hat{I}_{rgb} = \hat{H} \Phi$$
This layer acts as a strict structural filter, forcing the network to explicitly acknowledge how its spectral guess will be integrated by the specific camera sensor.

\subsubsection{Physics-Informed Loss Function with Adaptive Weighting}
To train the network, I propose a joint optimization strategy evaluating both the augmented data prior and physical inconsistencies. The total loss function $\mathcal{L}_{total}$ is defined as:
$$\mathcal{L}_{total} = \mathcal{L}_{recon}(\hat{H}, H'_{gt}) + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{physics}(\hat{I}_{rgb}, I_{rgb})$$
where $\lambda$ is an adaptively weighted hyperparameter. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Data Prior Loss ($\mathcal{L}_{recon}$):} I will utilize the $L1$ Loss (Mean Absolute Error), as standard MSE struggles with metameric gradients, to ensure the predicted spectrum closely matches the augmented ground truth $H'_{gt}$.
    \item \textbf{Physics Constraint Loss ($\mathcal{L}_{physics}$):} Computed between the input RGB image $I_{rgb}$ and the simulated RGB image $\hat{I}_{rgb}$. Minimizing this mathematically restricts the network's prediction to a valid metamer under the provided specific SRF constraint.
    \item \textbf{Adaptive Weighting:} Rather than using a static scalar for $\lambda$, the optimizer will dynamically adjust the weight based on the ratio of the gradients between the two loss terms, preventing the data prior from overwhelming the physical constraints.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
To comprehensively evaluate the reconstruction accuracy and physical validity, the following metrics will be utilized: Mean Relative Absolute Error (MRAE), Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR), and Spectral Angle Mapper (SAM) to specifically evaluate spectral shape fidelity. Computational performance will be evaluated based on model parameter count and inference time.
